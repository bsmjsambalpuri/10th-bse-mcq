A Letter to God

Chapter: A Letter to God

Author: G. L. Fuentes

Story

Lencho, a hardworking farmer described as an "ox of a man," lived in a solitary house on the crest of a low hill in a valley. His field of ripe corn needed rain, and when big drops finally began to fall, he joyfully compared them to "new coins". However, the rain quickly turned into a violent hailstorm that lasted for an hour, leaving the field white as if covered with salt and totally destroying the corn.

Despite his despair, Lencho had unwavering faith in God and believed that God's eyes see everything, even deep in one's conscience. He wrote a letter "To God," asking for a hundred pesos to resow his field and support his family.

At the post office, the postmaster—a fat, amiable fellow—was so moved by Lencho's faith that he decided to answer the letter. He collected seventy pesos from his employees and friends as an "act of charity" and sent it to Lencho, signing the letter simply: "God".

When Lencho received the money, he showed no surprise, but he became angry upon counting it and finding only seventy pesos. Believing God could not have made a mistake, he wrote a second letter asking for the remaining money.

He specifically requested God not to send it through the mail, because he believed the post office employees were a "bunch of crooks" who had stolen the missing thirty pesos.

Theme and Understanding

  • Unwavering Faith: The central theme explores the power of faith, illustrating how Lencho’s absolute belief in God provided him hope in a time of total loss.
  • The Irony of Human Nature: There is a profound irony in the conclusion; Lencho's blind faith leads him to distrust the very people (the postal workers) who made a personal sacrifice to help him.
  • Conflict between Faith and Reality: The story poses the question of what we should put our faith in, contrasting Lencho's spiritual certainty with the complex, sometimes misunderstood reality of human kindness.
