Chapter 2: Types of Sentences

A complete grammatical sentence requires at least one independent finite clause. Sentences are categorized based on their core structural finite clauses.

1. Simple Sentences

A simple sentence contains exactly one independent finite clause. It is subdivided into 4 functional types:

  • Declarative: Makes a simple structural statement. (e.g., "Cuttack is on the Mahanadi.")
  • Interrogative: Asks a question ending with a question mark. (e.g., "Is the cuckoo singing?")
  • Imperative: Expresses a command, advice, request, or suggestion. (e.g., "Listen to the cuckoo's song.")
  • Exclamatory: Expresses strong, sudden feelings. (e.g., "How beautifully the cuckoo sings!")

2. Compound Sentences

Structure Contains two or more independent coordinate clauses joined by coordinating conjunctions or correlatives.

  • Coordinators: and, but, or, so, yet, for, otherwise.
  • Correlatives (Pairs): either...or, neither...nor, not only...but also, both...and.
  • Example: He finished his exercises, and he put away his books.

3. Complex Sentences

Structure Composed of one independent main clause (M.C.) and one or more dependent subordinate clauses (S.C.).

Sentence Construction Blueprint:
├── Simple Sentence ──
├── Compound Sentence ──
└── Complex Sentence ──
├── Noun Clause
├── Relative / Adjective Clause
└── Adverb Clause

Example: I am certain that I shall complete the work today. (Subordinate Noun Clause acting as object/complement) .
